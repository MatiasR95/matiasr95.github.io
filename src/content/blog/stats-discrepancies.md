📊 Numbers almost never match perfectly — and that's okay. 🧮

The job isn't to chase the "perfect" figure. It's to understand the spread well enough to back ONE number the business can actually trust. Four simple stats do most of the heavy lifting 👇

📈 Mean (average) — great… until one outlier drags it. One huge value and your "average" describes nobody.
🎯 Median (the middle value) — the honest center when data is skewed. If extremes distort the mean, report the median.
📏 Standard deviation — how much things bounce around the average. Your "noise" meter. 🔊
⚖️ Coefficient of Variation (CV = std ÷ mean) — the underrated one. It turns variability into a %, so you can fairly compare two datasets of totally different sizes.

Tiny example 🧪 — values [10, 11, 12, 13, 94]:
- Mean ≈ 28 → misleading 😬
- Median = 12 → much closer to reality ✅

The mean screams "something's off." The median + CV tell you how off, and whether it's stable enough to commit to. 🎯

So when the data's messy 👇
✅ Lead with the median when outliers distort the mean.
✅ Use CV (not raw std dev) to compare variability across different scales.
✅ Quantify the gap, project it over time, and report a range — not false precision.

You don't need perfect data. You need to understand its shape well enough to support a decision. 💡📈

### En español

📊 Los números casi nunca coinciden perfecto, y está bien. 🧮

La idea no es buscar el dato "perfecto", sino entender la dispersión para poder bancar UN número confiable.

📈 Media (promedio): útil… hasta que un outlier la arrastra.
🎯 Mediana: el centro honesto cuando los datos están sesgados.
📏 Desvío estándar: cuánto "ruido" hay alrededor del promedio. 🔊
⚖️ Coeficiente de variación (CV = desvío ÷ media): convierte la variabilidad en % y te deja comparar conjuntos de distinto tamaño.

Ejemplo 🧪 [10, 11, 12, 13, 94] → media ≈ 28 (engañosa 😬), mediana = 12 (más real ✅). Con outliers, reportá la mediana; para comparar variabilidad, usá CV, no el desvío solo. No necesitás datos perfectos: necesitás entender su forma. 💡📈
