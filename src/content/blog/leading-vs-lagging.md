📊 One of the most useful concepts in analytics, I picked up almost by accident — watching a side project run on autopilot.

I have a small gym I set up a while back. I automated most of it, so these days I mostly watch it from the outside, through its data: sign-ins, payments, how often each member shows up.

One pattern kept repeating. People didn't churn when they cancelled — they churned weeks earlier, when their visits quietly slipped from ~3 a week to 1. The cancellation was the lagging indicator. The drop in attendance was the leading one.

That's the difference between leading and lagging indicators — and it's the backbone of churn prediction and early-warning analytics:

- Lagging metrics confirm what already happened — revenue, cancellations, results.
- Leading metrics move first and buy you time to act — engagement, usage, frequency.
- The real skill is finding the leading signal that reliably predicts the lagging outcome.

Lately I'm leaning on AI to take it further — learning to set up tools that flag those early signals automatically, so something odd or worth a look surfaces before it becomes a problem, not after. Still learning, one experiment at a time. 📈

Tengo un gimnasio chico que armé hace un tiempo. Lo automaticé, así que hoy lo miro más desde afuera, a través de sus datos: ingresos, pagos, cuántas veces viene cada persona.

Esa es la diferencia entre indicadores adelantados y atrasados, y es la base de la predicción de churn: los atrasados confirman lo que ya pasó; los adelantados se mueven antes y te dan tiempo a reaccionar.
